the bad news

You are a terrible guesser.

I'm also a terrible guesser. As are most people, with the exception of some well-trained meterologists and professional gamblers.

'Guessing' here refers to a properly calibrated probability estimate. If one were to guess the gross domestic earnings of The Goonies, the answer would probably be wrong, not because of profound personal faults, but because this tests specific domain knowledge. Rephrasing the question as:

“What are your high and low estimates for the gross domestic earnings of the The Goonies? What interval estimate of confidence would give this range?”
The answer might be:
“Between $100,000 to $100,000,000, inclusive, with 90% confidence.”[note]
Given enough data points in a sufficiently ideal world, the estimated confidence interval would match the observed accuracy. In other words, the range estimates given with 90% confidence should – on average and with minimal variance – be correct 90% of the time. This changes the exercise from a measurement of trivia knowledge to a measurement of ability to gauge uncertainty. However, much fewer than 90% of answers with 90% confidence will contain the actual value, not because of a lack of public knowledge in 20th century film economics, but because humans habitually display overconfident estimates. What we know is about 10-35 percentage points less than what we think we know in this example[note].

cognitive bugs

There exists plenty of empirical research on the limits and prejudices of probability estimates, and a few theories on their potential causes, most of which can be found among other lists of cognitive biases.

  • Anchoring: the wording and structure of the questions decrease epistemic accuracy by 53 percentage points
  • Pattern-seeking habits of humans create the illusion of signal where there is only noisy data page 12.
  • Prospect theory: small probabilities are overweighted, especially when attached to high-consequence events 100-101..
  • We actively seek to reduce risk/uncertainty, and will pay an economic premium for it 100-101..
  • Our educational training emphasizes algebra and calculus as the end goals, not probability and statistics.
  • We ignore prior probabilities.
  • Many social systems reward overconfidence.
  • In situations determined primarily by chance, a narrative[note] is often built to coherently explain the events, often in hindsight. E.g., market fluctuations due to labor reports, portfolio performance due to investment strategies or combat effectiveness predicted by training exercises[note].
These biases are to estimation as geometrical-optical illusions are to psychometrics. A simple change of the problem context causes a predicatable change in the perceived reality. In general, humans have a very troubled relationship with uncertainty. We don't understand it instintually, we don't communicate it well and we're willing to pay Part I, Chapter II. to avoid it.

you should care

Remember that weekend project, sitting unfinished months later?

Or that time your proposal estimate was off by an order-of-magnitude?

Or when that investment turned out so badly it scared you back into bonds and CD's for next ten years?

Even if you don't live in a region with legalized gambling or work in a forecasting profession, everyday failures of estimation hurt your job and quality of life. You make decisions based on uncertainty and imperfect knowledge, knowing much less than you think you know. As far as ubiquitious problems of human existence, it's right up there with communicable disease[note].

More importantly, inability to accurately estimate closes the door to powerful tools of probablistic thinking. Bayesian prediction[note] avoids the nuances of frequentist statistics, while allowing your mental model to adapt as the facts changes. It's something for which the Army and Air Force train, and M.D.s understand through years of experience. Along with the distance-rate-time equation, time-value equation and logical equalities, it's one of the those unreasonably effective structures of math, which internalizing will vastly improve your thinking.

mensa mea bona est

Overconfidence follows a predictable pattern. Overconfidence is usual for difficult assessments (although slightly less for true/false tests p64.). In some cases, very easy questions inspire underconfidence. Calibration can help to correct this. A few recommend techniques are:

  • Consider the reasons why your judgment might be wrong p228..
  • Range estimates to reduce the anchoring effect of a point estimate. Particularly, working towards a narrow range for an absurdly large range p64-5..
Things that don't fix overconfidence:
  • More information. Paradoxically, providing more information to the problem increases one's confidence in the answer, but not accuracy.

Most importantly, feedback and iterative practice allow us to improve our estimation techniques, hence the purpose of this project. Select the number of questions you want and the quiq will provide give you instant feedback on your confidence as well as the correct answers.

50% confidence indicates that you have no idea which answer is correct. 100% indicates absolute certainty of the correct answer. The more questions you select, the more accurate your calibration will be.
